The Roots

To understand Palestine is to understand a history systematically targeted for erasure. The story does not begin in 1948, but in the soil and soul of a people whose dispossession was planned decades earlier. The British Mandate (1922-1948), established after the fall of the Ottoman Empire, was tasked with guiding Palestine to self-rule. Instead, it implemented the 1917 Balfour Declaration, promising a "national home for the Jewish people" in a land that was already 94% Palestinian Arab. This inherent contradiction set the stage for conflict, privileging the ambitions of a settler movement over the rights of the indigenous majority.

, at its core, is a 19th-century European settler-colonial project. Its foundational premise required the transformation of Palestine into a Jewish state, which could not be achieved without the displacement of the native population. Early Zionist leaders were explicit about this, viewing their work as a "colonization adventure." The lead-up to 1948 saw the systematic execution of this vision, culminating in the Nakba ("The Catastrophe").

The 1947 UN Partition Plan allocated 55% of Palestine to a Jewish state, despite Jews owning only around 7% of the land and comprising a third of the population. This triggered the 1948 war, during which Zionist militias and later the Israeli army depopulated over 500 Palestinian villages and cities, expelling at least 750,000 people and making them refugees. This was not a byproduct of war; it was the realization of a plan. The Roots of this conflict are found in this violent, ongoing process of ethnic cleansing and land theft.

The Resistance

Resistance is the natural and righteous response to occupation and oppression. For Palestinians, it is a continuous, diverse, and deeply-rooted struggle for self-determination and return. It is not a monolith, but a dynamic combination of strategies, from the steadfastness of a farmer on his land to the global call for boycott. To understand the resistance is to understand the Palestinian will to exist.

The Nakba: An Ongoing Catastrophe

The Nakba, meaning "Catastrophe" in Arabic, refers to the systematic and violent ethnic cleansing of Palestine by Zionist militias and later the Israeli army to establish the state of Israel. This was not a byproduct of war; it was a deliberate plan executed before, during, and after the 1948 war.

Core Realities of the 1948 Nakba:

  • Mass Expulsion: At least 750,000 Palestinians—more than half the indigenous Arab population—were forcibly expelled from their homes.
  • Village Destruction: Over 530 Palestinian villages and towns were systematically destroyed and depopulated to prevent the return of their inhabitants.
  • Massacres: Dozens of massacres were carried out to terrorize the population into fleeing, with the Deir Yassin massacre being one of the most infamous examples.

The Nakba is Not Over:

The Nakba is an ongoing process of colonial violence. It continues today through:

  • The illegal military occupation of the West Bank and Gaza Strip.
  • The construction of illegal settlements and the apartheid wall.
  • The demolition of Palestinian homes and confiscation of land.
  • The denial of the legally-enshrined Right of Return for Palestinian refugees (UN Resolution 194).
"The old will die and the young will forget." — David Ben-Gurion (Israel's first Prime Minister), expressing the Zionist hope for the erasure of Palestinian memory.

Deconstructing Zionism

Zionism must be meticulously dissected as a racist, settler-colonial ideology. It is not synonymous with Judaism, which is a diverse religion and culture. Anti-Zionism is a political critique of a state ideology, not bigotry against Jewish people.

Core Tenets of Analysis:

  • Historical Development: Zionism arose as a 19th-century European political movement. It relied on the violent dispossession and ethnic cleansing of the indigenous Palestinian people to achieve its goal of a Jewish-majority state.
  • Settler-Colonialism: It functions by replacing the indigenous population with a settler society, seizing land and resources, and creating a legal and political structure that privileges the settler group.
  • Inherent Racism: By definition, Zionism requires a demographic majority and thus discriminates against non-Jews, codifying this discrimination in laws such as the 2018 Nation-State Law, which formalizes Jewish supremacy and establishes apartheid.
"Zionism is a colonizing venture and, therefore, it stands or falls on the question of armed forces." — Ze'ev Jabotinsky, 'The Iron Wall', 1923

The State of Israel is the practical manifestation of this ideology: an illegitimate apartheid regime and a primary agent of imperialism in the region. Neutralizing propaganda that conflates anti-Zionism with antisemitism is a critical task for all who struggle for justice.

Sumud: Steadfastness as Resistance

Sumud (صمود) is an Arabic word meaning "steadfastness" or "resilience." For Palestinians, it is a central strategy of non-violent resistance and a core cultural value. It is the refusal to be erased and the determination to remain on one's land despite immense pressure from the Israeli occupation.

Manifestations of Sumud:

  • Clinging to the Land: Farmers cultivating their olive groves in the face of settler attacks, and families rebuilding their homes after demolition are acts of Sumud.
  • Cultural Preservation: Cooking traditional food, wearing and creating Tatreez (embroidery), singing folk songs, and telling Palestinian stories are all ways of resisting cultural erasure.
  • Political & Civil Action: Participating in protests, documenting human rights abuses, and maintaining social institutions in the face of occupation are forms of collective Sumud.
  • Nurturing Life: Simply raising a family, educating children, and celebrating life under occupation is a powerful statement of resilience and a rejection of despair.
"Our strategy is to be rooted in the land, as deep as the olive tree. To exist is to resist."
